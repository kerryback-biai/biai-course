From Personal Productivity to Enterprise Data

This section divider opens the bridge segment (minutes 0-8). You are transitioning the class from “personal AI” mode — where they spent a week generating charts, documents, and workflows with Claude — into “enterprise AI” mode, where the same technology connects to corporate databases. Let the title land, then advance.

The Shift

You have spent a week using AI for personal productivity — charts, documents, workflows. Now we scale up. The same AI that drafts your memos can also query your company’s databases.

But enterprise data is scattered across 5, 10, sometimes 20 systems. Today we see what that looks like — and what AI can do about it.

Say: “You have spent a week using AI for personal productivity — charts, documents, workflows. You have iterated, critiqued, and built reusable prompts. Now we scale up. The same AI that drafts your memos can also query your company’s databases. But enterprise data is scattered across many systems. Today we see what that looks like and what AI can do about it.”

“By the end of this session, you will have queried enterprise databases in plain English, watched AI merge data across systems that don’t talk to each other, and started mapping your own company’s data landscape. Let’s jump right in.”

Transition: advance to the cold open.

The 3-Week Email Chain

This is the cold open provocation (minutes 8-13). The goal is to create an emotional anchor: the frustration of waiting weeks for a simple cross-system question. You will present the scenario and then immediately contrast it with the XYZ Corp app demo.

“Which of Our Customers Buy from Multiple Divisions?”

The CEO’s Question

  • Simple enough to ask in one sentence
  • Requires data from 3 separate CRMs
  • Nobody owns the cross-system view
  • The answer should drive pricing strategy

The 3-Week Reality

  • Week 1: BI team extracts from Salesforce
  • Week 2: Energy team pulls from legacy CRM
  • Week 3: Manual matching in Excel, finally delivered
  • CEO has moved on to a different priority

Say: “Here is a scenario that might feel familiar. A CEO asks a simple question: which of our customers buy from multiple divisions? It is one sentence. It should be answerable. But here is what actually happens.”

Walk through the right card: “Week one, the BI team extracts data from Salesforce. Week two, someone from the Energy division pulls from their legacy CRM. Week three, an analyst is manually matching customer names in Excel. By the time the answer arrives, the CEO has moved on to a different priority.”

Ask the room: “Has this happened to you? A simple business question that took weeks because the data lived in different systems? Raise your hand if the answer is yes.”

Pause for responses. Then: “What I am about to show you takes about 30 seconds.”

Demo: Dashboard vs. Chat

This opens the live demo segment (minutes 13-23). Make sure the XYZ Corp app is open in another browser tab and ready. You are about to ask the same cross-system question and get an instant answer. This is the “wow” moment of the session.

Introducing XYZ Corp

The Simulation Environment

  • $500M B2B industrial distributor with 3 operating divisions
  • 10 enterprise systems: Salesforce, Legacy CRM, HubSpot, NetSuite, SAP, Oracle SCM, Workday, Zendesk, QuickBooks
  • 41 tables, 26,000+ rows, 3 years of data
  • Realistic cross-system friction: different naming conventions, date formats, column styles

You will log in and query this environment yourself in 15 minutes. First, watch what happens when we ask it the CEO’s question.

Briefly introduce the XYZ Corp simulation. Say: “This is the environment you will be working with for the rest of the course. XYZ Corp is a fictional $500M B2B industrial supplies distributor. It has three divisions — Industrial, Energy, and Safety — each with their own CRM, plus shared finance, HR, and support systems. Ten systems total. Forty-one database tables. Three years of realistic data.”

“The key word is realistic. The systems have different naming conventions. The Legacy CRM stores dates as text strings. Customers appear under different names across divisions — General Electric in one system, GE Industrial Solutions in another. This is what real enterprise data looks like.”

“You will log in and query this yourself in a few minutes. But first, let me show you what happens when we ask it the CEO’s question.”

Switch to the XYZ Corp app and prepare for the live demo.

Live Demo: “Which Customers Buy from Multiple Divisions?”

Walk through each step card. Say: “What you just saw was not a single database query. The agent queried three separate systems, one at a time. It pulled customer data and revenue from Salesforce, then the Legacy CRM, then HubSpot. Then it used Python to merge the results.”

“The merge step is where the magic happens. The agent used fuzzy name matching to link ‘General Electric’ in Salesforce to ‘GE Industrial Solutions’ in the Legacy CRM to ‘GE Safety Division’ in HubSpot. An exact-match join would have missed 7 of the 10 multi-division customers.”

“This is important to understand: the agent cannot join across systems in a single SQL query. It queries each system where the data lives, then reconciles in code. This mirrors how a real enterprise AI tool would work — and it is different from a data warehouse, where you would move everything into one place first.”

The Implication

Dashboards

  • Pre-built views of pre-selected metrics
  • Cannot answer questions they were not designed for
  • Every new question requires a development cycle

BI Teams

  • Skilled analysts writing SQL and building reports
  • Bottleneck: 3-week turnaround for cross-system questions
  • High value, but limited bandwidth

AI Agents

  • Any question, any time, in plain English
  • Cross-system queries with automatic reconciliation
  • Not a replacement — a force multiplier

Walk through each card. Say: “Dashboards are great for monitoring known metrics. But they cannot answer questions they were not designed for. Every new question requires a development cycle.”

“BI teams are highly skilled. But they are a bottleneck. The 3-week email chain is not because anyone is bad at their job — it is because there are too many questions and too few analysts.”

“AI agents sit in a different category. Any question, any time, in plain English. But I want to be clear: this is not a replacement for dashboards or BI teams. It is a force multiplier. Dashboards still monitor KPIs. BI teams still build validated data models. The agent handles the ad hoc questions that today go unanswered or take weeks.”

Transition: “Now it is your turn. Let me get you logged into the XYZ Corp app.”

Workshop: Your Company’s Data Map

This opens the data map workshop (minutes 53-63). Students shift from the XYZ Corp simulation to thinking about their own companies. This work carries forward through the rest of the course and into the capstone.

Map Your Systems

Individual Exercise (10 minutes)

  1. List your company’s 3-5 core systems (CRM, ERP, HR, finance, support, etc.)
  2. For each system: What data lives there? Who owns it? How do you access it today?
  3. Identify one cross-system question nobody can answer easily today
  4. Estimate: Who answers it now? How long does it take?

This data map is the foundation for your capstone strategy. Be specific — system names, not categories.

Say: “Now I want you to think about your own company. Take 10 minutes and map your data landscape. List your 3 to 5 core systems — not categories like ‘CRM,’ but actual system names. Salesforce. SAP. Workday. Whatever your company uses.”

“For each system, note what data lives there and who owns it. Then identify one cross-system question that nobody can answer easily today. The kind of question that, like the CEO’s question about multi-division customers, sounds simple but requires pulling data from multiple systems.”

“This data map carries forward. It becomes the foundation for your capstone strategy in Session 8. Be specific.”

Give students the full 10 minutes. Circulate and help anyone who is stuck.

What We’re Skipping

This opens the expectations calibration segment (minutes 78-87). You are about to name everything the demo did NOT show — the pre-work required before any agent can query real systems. This is critical for preventing the “we can do this on Monday” reaction.

The 60-90 Days the Demo Didn’t Show

Data Cataloging

  • Inventory every table, column, and relationship
  • Document what each field actually means
  • Map data quality issues

Schema Documentation

  • Write descriptions the LLM can understand
  • The system prompt is “institutional knowledge”
  • Gets it wrong? Fix the schema description

API Access

  • Provision read-only database connections
  • Set up authentication and rate limits
  • Security review for each system

PII Classification

  • Identify sensitive fields (salary, SSN, health data)
  • Define access control rules per role
  • Decide what the LLM is allowed to see

Say: “Before we wrap, I need to calibrate expectations. Everything you saw today was impressive. But the XYZ Corp demo hides something important: the 60 to 90 days of pre-work that happened before the first question was ever asked.”

Walk through each card: “Someone had to catalog every table and column. Someone had to write schema descriptions the LLM can understand — that system prompt is not automatic, it is carefully authored institutional knowledge. Someone had to provision API access to each system with proper security. And someone had to classify every field for PII sensitivity and define access control rules.”

“This pre-work is real. It is not glamorous. And it is the difference between a demo and a production deployment. I name it now so that when you go back to your organizations and say ‘I saw something amazing,’ you also say ‘and here is what it takes to get there.’”

How Data Moves Today

The Traditional Answer: Data Warehouse

  • Move all data into one place (Snowflake, BigQuery, Redshift)
  • Cost: $500K–$5M+ to build
  • Timeline: 6–18 months
  • Advantage: one source of truth, fast queries
  • Downside: stale data, expensive, slow to change

The Alternative: Query Where It Lives

  • AI agent queries each system via API
  • No data movement — always current
  • Cost: lower upfront, per-query costs
  • Advantage: fresh data, fast to deploy
  • Downside: slower queries, more complex orchestration

Most enterprises will use both — a warehouse for core reporting, agents for ad hoc cross-system questions.

Say: “There are two approaches to getting cross-system answers. The traditional answer is a data warehouse — move everything into one place. This is proven technology. It works. But it costs $500K to $5 million, takes 6 to 18 months, and the data is always at least slightly stale.”

“The alternative is what you just saw: query each system where it lives. No data movement. Always current. Faster to deploy. But the queries are slower and the orchestration is more complex.”

“In practice, most enterprises will use both. A warehouse for core reporting and dashboards. Agents for ad hoc cross-system questions that the warehouse was not designed to answer.”

“We will dig deeper into deployment architecture in Session 5. For now, just know that both options exist and there are real tradeoffs.”
